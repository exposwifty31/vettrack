# Device Models

## Design Hierarchy

Start with human behavior, then usage context, then interaction model, then technical implementation. Avoid beginning with screen size or component fit.

## Device Cognition

| Device | Cognitive model | Attention economy | Primary design pressure |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| iPhone | Action cognition | Seconds | Speed, reach, interruption recovery |
| iPad | Workspace cognition | Concentration | Context, density, discoverability |
| Mac | Expertise cognition | Expertise | Precision, persistence, command depth |

The iPad is not a large iPhone. It combines Mac-like workflow cognition with touch-first physical interaction.

## Human Attention

iPhone sessions are often short, reactive, stressed, contextual, and externally driven. Design for 5-second, 20-second, 45-second, and 2-minute use.

iPad sessions can become focused, exploratory, analytical, and internally driven. Design for 10-minute, 30-minute, 90-minute, and multi-hour use.

## Cognitive Load

Humans can actively hold only a small number of concepts at once.

On iPhone, reduce cognitive load. Infer context, reduce options, and present the next action.

On iPad, externalize cognitive load. Keep related patient, vitals, medication, equipment, lab, billing, and operational context visible when the workflow benefits from comparison.

## Information Architecture

Use sequential architecture for phone flows, onboarding, checkout, and urgent single-path tasks.

Use hierarchical architecture for settings and structured navigation.

Use network architecture for professional tools and hospital software where entities influence each other.

Use spatial architecture for iPad and desktop workspaces with persistent regions such as sidebars, inspectors, toolbars, and panels.

## Interaction Laws

Fitts's Law: movement time rises with distance and falls with target size. On iPhone, keep targets large, close, and thumb-reachable. On iPad, distance matters less than consistent placement, discoverability, and density.

Hick's Law: decision time rises with choice count. On iPhone, reduce choices. On iPad, group choices into inspectors, panels, and toolbars.

Gestalt grouping matters more as density rises. Group fields and actions by clinical meaning before optimizing individual components.

Spatial memory is a first-class iPad affordance. Users remember "vitals are on the right" more reliably than nested menu positions.

## Motor Control

iPhone interaction is thumb-led: fast, imprecise, and one-handed. Favor large controls, low reach, and immediate feedback.

iPad interaction can combine finger, pointer, trackpad, Pencil, and keyboard. Favor repeatable actions, precision affordances, keyboard shortcuts, and multi-select where workflows justify them.

## Error Recovery

iPhone philosophy: prevent errors through constraints, confirmation, disabled invalid actions, and plain-language warnings.

iPad philosophy: allow professional exploration with recovery through undo, redo, version history, snapshots, rollback, and review states.

## Feature Matrix Prompts

Ask "should this feature exist on this device?" before asking whether it can fit.

Useful iPhone candidates: QR scan, quick medication administration, emergency response, simple approval, status check, call/message, and immediate recovery.

Useful iPad candidates: bulk editing, keyboard shortcuts, multiwindow, workspace saving, extensive drag/drop, advanced dashboards, batch operations, shift handoff, and multi-patient monitoring.

## Success Dimensions

Functional: can users complete the task?

Behavioral: do users naturally use the feature?

Cognitive: can users understand it without carrying hidden state in memory?

Emotional: do users trust the surface under stress or over long sessions?

Economic: does it improve clinical or business outcomes?
